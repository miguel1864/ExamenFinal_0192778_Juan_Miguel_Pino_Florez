Project Objective

The goal of this project is to help everyday internet users understand how their personal data is exposed online, why that exposure matters, and what concrete steps reduce the risk. Most people do not lack interest in privacy; they lack the information needed to recognize a threat and the tools needed to react to it. This site addresses that gap by combining three things: an explanation of the most common attacks, real statistics that show the scale of the problem, and two interactive tools (a risk checker and a password analyzer) that let visitors evaluate their own digital habits instead of just reading about them. The project is built with HTML5 for structure, CSS3 for the visual design, and JavaScript for the interactive logic, and it closes with a short quiz to verify that the key concepts were understood.

The Problem

Every day, millions of people are affected by phishing attacks, malware infections, identity theft, and password leaks. The root cause is rarely a sophisticated hack: it is usually a reused password, a click on a suspicious link, or sensitive information shared without a second thought. Malware

Identity Theft

Conclusion

Data privacy is one of the most pressing challenges of the digital age, affecting individuals, companies, and educational institutions alike. As shown throughout this project, the problem is not abstract: stolen credentials, phishing, and weak passwords account for a large share of real breaches reported by organizations like Verizon and IBM, and Colombia specifically faces a high volume of fraud and phishing incidents every year. The solution does not require advanced technical knowledge. Using unique passwords, recognizing suspicious links, keeping software updated, and understanding how attacks work are simple habits that close most of the gap exploited by attackers. The interactive tools on this page (the risk checker, the password analyzer, and the quiz) were designed precisely to turn that theoretical knowledge into a habit the visitor can practice. This project demonstrates the application of HTML5, CSS3 and JavaScript while addressing a real-world problem with data-backed evidence and a practical, educational solution.
